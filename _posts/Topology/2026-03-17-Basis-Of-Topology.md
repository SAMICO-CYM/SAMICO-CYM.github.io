## Definition
Let $X$ be a set. A ***basis*** for a topology on $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}$ of subsets of $X$ such that

**(i)** For each $x \in X$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B$.

**(ii)** If $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$ for $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$, then there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_3 \subset B_1 \cap B_2$.

선형대수적인 센스에서 생각해보자. 벡터 공간의 각 원소

---

## Remark

**(i)** Each basis element $B$ of $\mathcal{B}$ is an element of $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$.

**(ii)** Every topology on a set $X$ is a basis. But the converse is **NOT** true in general.

$(\because)$ Let $\mathcal{T}$ be a topology on a set $X$. For each $x \in X$, $x \in X \in \mathcal{T}$. If $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$ for $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{T}$, then $x \in B_1 \cap B_2 \in \mathcal{T}$. Thus $\mathcal{T}$ is a basis for a topology on $X$. $\blacksquare$ 


---
## Example

**(i)** The collection 

$$\mathcal{B}_1 = \{ (a, b) \mid a, b \in \overline{\mathbb{R}} \}$$

of open intervals in $\mathbb{R}$ is a basis for a topology on $\mathbb{R}$, but it is **NOT** a topology on $\mathbb{R}$.

**(ii)** Let $X$ be a set. The collection

$$\mathcal{B}_2 = \{ \{ x \} \mid x \in X \}$$

is a basis for the discrete topology on $X$.

## Theorem 1

Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a basis for a topology on $X$. Then the collection 

$$\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}} = \{ U \subset X \mid \forall x \in U, \exists B_x \in \mathcal{B} \, [x \in B_x \subset U] \}$$

is a topology on $X$, and we say that $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$ is ***the topology generated by $\mathcal{B}$.*** 

즉 부분집합 $U \subset X$가 기저 집합들에 대해서 open set의 성질을 만족하도록 collection을 구성하면 자연스럽게 위상 공간을 만들 수 있고, 이를 기저 $\mathcal{B}$가 생성하는 위상 공간이라고 부른다. 

### Proof
If $U = \emptyset$, then it satisfies the condition vacuously, which means that $\emptyset \in \mathcal{T}$. Let $U = X$ and let $x \in U$. Since $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis, $\exists B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset X = U$, so that $X \in \mathcal{T}$.

Let $\\{ U_\alpha \\}$ be a subcollection of $\mathcal{T}$. Then clearly the union of the subcollection is a subset of $X$. If we choose arbitrary $x$ in this union, then $x \in U_\alpha$ for some $U_\alpha \in \mathcal{T}$. By the definition, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset U_\alpha \subset \bigcup U_\alpha$. Thus the union of the subcollection is an element of $\mathcal{T}$.

Let $\\{U_i \\}_{i=1}^n$ be a finite subcollection of $\mathcal{T}$.

## Theorem 2
Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a basis on a set $X$, and let $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$ be the topology on $X$ generated by $\mathcal{B}$. Then 

$$\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}} = \left\{ \bigcup_{U \in \mathcal{A}} U \,\, \Bigg \vert \,\, \mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B}   \right\}$$

선형대수적인 센스에서 생각해보자. 벡터 공간의 임의의 원소는 기저벡터들의 선형 결합으로 표현할 수 있었듯이, 기저 $\mathcal{B}$가 생성하는 위상 공간의 임의의 open set은 기저 집합들의 합집합으로 표현할 수 있다. 

### Proof 
Let us denote 

$$T = \left\{ \bigcup_{U \in \mathcal{A}} U \,\, \Bigg \vert \,\, \mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B} \right\}.$$

Let $\displaystyle \bigcup_{U \in \mathcal{A}} U \in T$ where $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B}$. Since each $U \in \mathcal{B}$ is an element of $\mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}}$ and $\mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}}$ is a topology on $X$, the union $\displaystyle \bigcup_{U \in \mathcal{A}} U$ belongs to $\mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}}$, which means that $T \subset \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$.

Let $U \in \mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}}$. If $x \in U$, then $\exists B_x \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_x \subset U$. Note that 

$$U = \bigcup_{x \in U} B_x.$$

Since $\\{ B_x \in \mathcal{B} \mid x \in U \\}$ is a subcollection of $\mathcal{B}$, we have $U \in T$, which means that $\mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}} \subset T$. Thus $T = \mathcal{T_\mathcal{B}}. \blacksquare$

## Theorem 3
Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space, and let $\mathcal{B}$ be a subcollection of $\mathcal{T}$. TFAE.

**(i)** $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$. That is, $\mathcal{T} = \mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$.

**(ii)** For each $U \in \mathcal{T}$ and $x \in U$, $\exists B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset U$.

### Proof
**(i)** $\implies$ **(ii)**

By definition of $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$, it is clear.

**(ii)** $\implies$ **(i)**

First, we show that $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis.

Let $x \in X$. Since $\mathcal{T}$ is a topology on $X$, $X \in \mathcal{T}$. Thus $\exists U \in \mathcal{T}$ such that $x \in U$. By assumption, $\exists B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset U$. 

Let $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$ for $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$. Since $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{T}$, so that $B_1 \cap B_2 \in \mathcal{T}$, $\exists B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_3 \subset B_1 \cap B_2$. 

Thus $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis.

Next, we show that $\mathcal{T} = \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$. 

Let $U \in \mathcal{T}$. If $x \in U$, $\exists B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset U$ by assumption. Thus $U \in \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$ by definition of $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$.

Let $U \in \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$. By Theorem 2, $U = \bigcup_{K \in \mathcal{A}} K$ for some $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B}$. Since $\mathcal{B} \subset \mathcal{T}$, we have $U \in \mathcal{T}$.

Thus $\mathcal{T} = \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}. \blacksquare$

## Theorem 4
Let $\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{B}'$ be bases for topologies $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$ and $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$, respectively, on a set $X$. TFAE.

**(i)** $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}} \subset \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$, that is, $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$ is finer than $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$.

**(ii)** For each $x \in X$ and $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B$, $\exists B' \in \mathcal{B}'$ such that $x \in B' \subset B$.

### Proof
**(i)** $\implies$ **(ii)**
Let $x \in X$ and let $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B$. Since $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}} \subset \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$, we have $B \in \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$. By definition of $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$, $\exists B' \in \mathcal{B}'$ such that $x \in B' \subset B$. 

**(ii)** $\implies$ **(i)**
Let $U \in \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$. For each $x \in X$, $\exists B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subset U$ by definition of $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}}$. By assumption, $\exists B' \in \mathcal{B}'$ such that $x \in B' \subset B \subset U$. Thus $U \in \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}$. Hence $\mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}}} \subset \mathcal{T_{\mathcal{B}'}}. \blacksquare$
