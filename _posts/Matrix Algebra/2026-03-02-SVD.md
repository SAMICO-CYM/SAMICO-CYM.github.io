## Lemma 1
Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$. Then the followings hold:
**(i)** The matrix $A^{\ast} A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ is Hermitian and positive-semidefinite, i.e., it has $n$ nonnegative real eigenvalues, say 

$$\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_r > 0, \, \, \sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n = 0$$

belonging to eigenvectors 

$$\mathbf{v_1}, \mathbf{v_2}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_r}, \mathbf{v_{r+1}}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_n},$$

respectively, which are chosen to be orthonormal. Then the eigenvectors $\mathbf{v_1}, \mathbf{v_2}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_r}$ of $A^{\ast}A$ belonging to positive eigenvalues form an orthonormal basis for the row space $\mathcal{R}(A) = \mathcal{R}(A^{\ast}A)$ in $\mathbb{C}^n$, where $\operatorname{rank}(A) = r$, and the eigenvectors $\mathbf{v_{r+1}}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_n}$ belonging to zero eigenvalue form an orthonormal basis for the null space $\mathcal{N}(A)$.
**(ii)** For positive $\sigma_i, i = 1, 2, ..., r$, set 

$$\mathbf{u_i} = \frac{A\mathbf{v_i}}{\sqrt{\sigma_i}}.$$

Then the vectors $\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r$ form an orthonormal basis for the column space $\mathcal{C}(A)( = \mathcal{R}(A^{T}) = \mathcal{R}(AA^{T}))$. Hence 

$$\sqrt{\sigma_i} = \| A \mathbf{v}_i \|.$$

**(iii)** The unit vectors $\mathbf{v}_i \in \mathcal{R}(A)$ and $\mathbf{u}_i \in \mathcal{C}(A)$ are related by the following equations: 

$$A \mathbf{v}_i = \sqrt{\sigma_i} \mathbf{u}_i \quad \text{and} \quad A^{\ast}\mathbf{u}_i = \sqrt{\sigma_i} \mathbf{v}_i, \quad i = 1, 2, ..., r.$$

Thus the vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, ..., \mathbf{v}_r$ are the eigenvectors of $A^{\ast}A$ belonging to positive eigenvalues $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_r$. Similarly, the vectors $\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r$ are the eigenvectors of $AA^{\ast}$ belonging to positive eigenvalues $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_r$.

### Proof
**(i)** Since $(A^{\ast}A)^{\ast} = A^{\ast}A$, $A^{\ast}A$ is Hermitian. For $x \in \mathbb{C}^n$, 

$$x^{\ast}A^{\ast}Ax = (Ax)^{\ast}Ax = \langle Ax, Ax \rangle = \| Ax \|^2 \ge 0,$$

which means that $A^{\ast}A$ is positive-semidefinite. Thus $A^{\ast}A$ is unitarily diagonalizable, so that it has $n$ nonnegative real eigenvalues. Since $\operatorname{rank}(A) = r$, there are eigenvalues $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_r, \sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n = 0$ (in descending order). By the unitarily diagonalizablity of $A^{\ast}A$ and the Gram-Schmidt process, we obtain the orthonormal basis $\\{ \mathbf{v_1}, \mathbf{v_2}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_r} \\}$ for $\mathcal{R}(A^{\ast}A) = \mathcal{R}(A)$ and the orthonormal basis $\\{ \mathbf{v_{r+1}}, \cdots, \mathbf{v_n} \\}$ for $\mathcal{N}(A^{\ast}A) = \mathcal{N}(A)$.

**(ii)** Clearly each vector $\mathbf{u_i} = \frac{A\mathbf{v_i}}{\sqrt{\sigma_i}}$ is nonzero and is contained in $\mathcal{C}(A)$ for $i = 1, ..., r$. Furthermore, we have 

$$\begin{align*} \langle \mathbf{u_i}, \mathbf{u_j} \rangle & = \left \langle \frac{A\mathbf{v_i}}{\sqrt{\sigma_i}}, \frac{A\mathbf{v_j}}{\sqrt{\sigma_j}} \right \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma_i} \sqrt{\sigma_j}} \langle A \mathbf{v_i}, A \mathbf{v_j} \rangle \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma_i} \sqrt{\sigma_j}} \langle A^{\ast} A \mathbf{v_i},\mathbf{v_j} \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma_i} \sqrt{\sigma_j}} \langle \sigma_i \mathbf{v_i}, \mathbf{v_j} \rangle \\ &= \frac{\sigma_i}{\sqrt{\sigma_i} \sqrt{\sigma_j}} \langle \mathbf{v_i}, A \mathbf{v_j} \rangle = \delta_{ij}, \end{align*}$$

which means that $\\{ \mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r \\}$ is an orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{C}(A).$ 

**(iii)** Note that 

$$A^{\ast} \mathbf{u_i} = \frac{ A^{\ast}A \mathbf{v_i}}{\sqrt{\sigma_i}} = \sqrt{\sigma_i} \mathbf{v_i}, \forall i \in \\{1, ..., r \\}.$$

Thus the vectors $\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r$ are the eigenvectors of $AA^{\ast}$ belonging to positive eigenvalues $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_r$. $\blacksquare$

## Definition 1
- Using the notation of the above lemma, the square roots $\sqrt{\sigma_1}, \sqrt{\sigma_2}, ..., \sqrt{\sigma_r}$ of positive eigenvalues of $A^{\ast}A$ are called ***singular values*** of a matrix $A$. 
- The basis vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, ..., \mathbf{v}_r$ for $\mathcal{R}(A)$ are called the ***right singular vectors*** of $A$. Similarly, basis vectors $\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r$ for $\mathcal{C}(A)$ are called the ***left singular vectors*** of $A$.

## Singular Value Decomposition
- Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$ with singular values $\sqrt{\sigma}_1 \ge \sqrt{\sigma}_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sqrt{\sigma}_r$ in descending order, and corresponding right singular vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, ..., \mathbf{v}_r$, and left singular vectors $\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, ..., \mathbf{u}_r$. Then the ***singular value decomposition*** of $A$ is given by 

$$A = U \Sigma V^* = \sum_{i=1}^r \sqrt{\sigma_i} \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^*$$

### Proof
Set 

$$\begin{gather*} V = \begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ \mathbf{v_1} & \cdots & \mathbf{v_r} & \mathbf{v_{r+1}} & \cdots & \mathbf{v_n} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad U = \begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ \mathbf{u_1} & \cdots & \mathbf{u_r} & \mathbf{u_{r+1}} & \cdots & \mathbf{u_n} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ \text{and} \quad  \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{\sigma_1} & & & & & \mathbf{0} \\ & \ddots & & & \\ & & \sqrt{\sigma_r} & & \\ & & & 0 & & \\ & & & & \ddots & & \\ \mathbf{0} & & & & & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{gather*}$$

Since $V$ and $U$ consist of orthonormal vectors, they are Hermitian.

Since $A\mathbf{v_i} = \sqrt{\sigma_i} \mathbf{u_i}, \forall i \in \\{1, ..., r \\}$ and $A \mathbf{v_i} = \mathbf{0}, \forall i \in \\{ r+1, ..., n \\},$ we have 

$$\begin{align*} AV & = A\begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ \mathbf{v_1} & \cdots & \mathbf{v_r} & \mathbf{v_{r+1}} & \cdots & \mathbf{v_n} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ A\mathbf{v_1} & \cdots & A\mathbf{v_r} & A\mathbf{v_{r+1}} & \cdots & A\mathbf{v_n} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ \sqrt{\sigma_1} \mathbf{u_1} & \cdots & \sqrt{\sigma_r} \mathbf{u_r} & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} \\ & = \begin{bmatrix} \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \\ \mathbf{u_1} & \cdots & \mathbf{u_r} & \mathbf{u_{r+1}} & \cdots & \mathbf{u_n} \\ \vert &  & \vert & \vert & & \vert \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{\sigma_1} & & & & & \mathbf{0} \\ & \ddots & & & \\ & & \sqrt{\sigma_r} & & \\ & & & 0 & & \\ & & & & \ddots & & \\ \mathbf{0} & & & & & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ &= U \Sigma, \end{align*}$$

which means that $A = U \Sigma V^{\ast}$. $\blacksquare$

## Remark
- One can check the identity $A = U\Sigma V^{\ast}$ by a direct multiplication of these three matrices. The matrices $U$ and $V$ are unitary, so each is a rotation or a reflection as a linear transformation, and $\Sigma$ is rectangular diagonal, so is a stretching/shrinking along each coordinate axis. It is shown that the matrix $A = U\Sigma V^{\ast}$ is a composition of the type Rotation (or reflection) $\circ$ Stretching $\circ$ Rotation (or reflection).
